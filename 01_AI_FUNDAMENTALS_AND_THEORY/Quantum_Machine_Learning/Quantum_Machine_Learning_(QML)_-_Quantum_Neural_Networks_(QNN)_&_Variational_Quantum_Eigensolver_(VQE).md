# Quantum Machine Learning (QML) - Quantum Neural Networks (QNN) & Variational Quantum Eigensolver (VQE)

## Description

Quantum Machine Learning (QML) represents the convergence of quantum computing and machine learning. Quantum Neural Networks (QNNs) are sophisticated computational models that combine principles of quantum mechanics, such as superposition and entanglement, with classical neural network architectures. They are designed to harness the expressive power of high-dimensional Hilbert spaces for tasks such as classification and feature extraction. The Variational Quantum Eigensolver (VQE) is a prominent hybrid quantum-classical algorithm that uses the Ritz variational principle to estimate the fundamental eigenvalue (ground-state energy) of a Hamiltonian. VQE is one of the most promising algorithms for near-term quantum computers (NISQ - Noisy Intermediate-Scale Quantum), because it delegates parameter optimization to a classical computer, mitigating the need for deep quantum circuits and tolerating a certain level of noise.

## Statistics

Recent research demonstrates that QNNs, especially in hybrid architectures such as Quantum-Classical-Quantum Convolutional Neural Networks (QCCNNs), can outperform classical CNNs in image classification accuracy under comparable architectures [1]. VQE, while prone to barren plateau problems, is continuously being improved; variants such as the Variational Quantum-Neural Hybrid Eigensolver (VQNHE) have been shown to significantly outperform standard VQE in simulating ground-state energies of quantum spins and molecules [3]. The efficiency of QNNs is often measured by their ability to reduce resource costs, requiring a smaller number of qubits for certain operations [1].

## Features

QNNs: 1. **Enhanced Expressiveness** due to the use of high-dimensional Hilbert spaces and entanglement. 2. **Hybrid Architecture** that integrates parameterized quantum circuits (VQC) as feature extraction layers or classifiers. 3. **Classical Optimization** of parameters via gradient descent. VQE: 1. **Hybrid Algorithm** that combines a quantum circuit (ansatz) to prepare the trial state and a classical optimizer to minimize the cost function (expectation value of the Hamiltonian). 2. **Ritz Variational Principle** to guarantee that the expectation value is an upper bound for the ground-state energy. 3. **Adaptability** through the choice of different ansätze (for example, RealAmplitudes, Quantum Unitary Coupled Cluster - Q-UCC) for different problems.

## Use Cases

QNNs: 1. **Image Classification** and pattern recognition [1]. 2. **Molecular Property Prediction** and drug discovery [4]. 3. **Sentiment Analysis** that is multimodal and enhanced classification [5]. VQE: 1. **Quantum Chemistry** to calculate ground-state energies of molecules (for example, H2, LiH) [2] [6]. 2. **Quantum Simulations** of many-body systems. 3. **Optimization Problems** and search for eigenvalues of Hamiltonians.

## Integration

The integration of QNNs and VQE is typically carried out through QML software frameworks that provide interfaces to build parameterized quantum circuits (VQC) and connect them to classical optimizers. **PennyLane** and **Qiskit** are the most widely used platforms. PennyLane, for example, allows VQE to be built concisely by defining the Hamiltonian, the quantum circuit (QNode), and the cost function, and using classical optimizers such as `optax.sgd` to minimize the energy. \n\n**Example of VQE with PennyLane (Python):**\n```python\nimport pennylane as qml\nfrom pennylane import numpy as np\nimport optax\n\n# 1. Define the device (simulator or hardware)\ndev = qml.device(\"default.qubit\", wires=4)\n\n# 2. Define the Hamiltonian (example: H2 at 0.74 Å separation)\n# H = -1.05 Z0 + 0.39 Z1 + 0.01 X0X1Y2Y3 + ... (simplified)\nH = qml.Hamiltonian(\n    [-1.05, 0.39, 0.01],\n    [qml.PauliZ(0), qml.PauliZ(1), qml.PauliX(0) @ qml.PauliX(1) @ qml.PauliY(2) @ qml.PauliY(3)]\n)\n\n# 3. Define the quantum circuit (Ansatz)\n@qml.qnode(dev, interface=\"jax\")\ndef circuit(param, wires):\n    qml.BasisState(np.array([1, 1, 0, 0]), wires=wires) # Hartree-Fock state\n    qml.DoubleExcitation(param, wires=[0, 1, 2, 3])\n    return qml.expval(H)\n\n# 4. Define the cost function\ndef cost_fn(param):\n    return circuit(param, wires=range(4))\n\n# 5. Classical Optimization\nmax_iterations = 100\nopt = optax.sgd(learning_rate=0.4)\ntheta = np.array(0.0)\nopt_state = opt.init(theta)\n\nfor n in range(max_iterations):\n    gradient = qml.grad(cost_fn)(theta)\n    updates, opt_state = opt.update(gradient, opt_state)\n    theta = optax.apply_updates(theta, updates)\n\nfinal_energy = cost_fn(theta)\n# The final_energy value is the estimate of the ground-state energy.\n```
