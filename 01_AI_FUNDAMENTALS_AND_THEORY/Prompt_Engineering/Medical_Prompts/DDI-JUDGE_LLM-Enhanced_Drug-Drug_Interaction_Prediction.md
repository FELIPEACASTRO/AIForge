# DDI-JUDGE: LLM-Enhanced Drug-Drug Interaction Prediction

## Description

**DDI-JUDGE** is an innovative method that uses Large Language Models (LLMs) for Drug-Drug Interaction (DDI) prediction, integrating **In-Context Learning (ICL)** and a **"Judging"** module. The method proposes a new ICL prompt paradigm that selects samples with high molecular similarity to optimize the model's learning. In addition, it employs a discriminator LLM (such as GPT-4) to assess the quality of the explanations generated by multiple LLMs and aggregates the results through weighted voting, increasing the robustness and accuracy of the prediction. This model represents a significant advance in the application of LLMs to pharmacological safety tasks.

## Statistics

**Performance Metrics (Zero-shot / Few-shot):**
- **AUC (Area Under the Curve):** 0.642 / 0.788
- **AUPR (Area Under the Precision-Recall Curve):** 0.629 / 0.801
- The method demonstrated superior performance compared to other LLM-based methods in both learning scenarios (zero-shot and few-shot).
- **Citation:** Qi et al. (2025). Improving drug-drug interaction prediction via in-context learning and judging with large language models. *Frontiers in Pharmacology*.

## Features

- **Optimized ICL:** Selection of ICL samples (positive and negative) based on molecular similarity (Tanimoto, Cosine, Dice) to guide the LLM.
- **Discriminator Module (GPT-4):** Assesses the scientific accuracy, clarity, evidence support, and relevance of the explanations generated by other LLMs.
- **Result Aggregation:** Combination of predictions from multiple LLMs using a confidence-weighted voting scheme.
- **Modular Structure:** Easily applicable to clinical workflows and other biomedical applications.

## Use Cases

- **DDI Prediction in Polypharmacy:** Identification of drug interactions in patients using multiple medications.
- **Clinical Decision Support:** Assisting pharmacists and physicians in the rapid, evidence-based assessment of DDI risks.
- **Pharmacological Safety Screening:** Application in early stages of drug discovery to predict potential adverse interactions.

## Integration

**Integration Guide (Zero-Shot Prompt):**

The prompt is structured to simulate a consultation with an experienced pharmacologist, requiring an analysis based on pharmacological factors and evidence.

**System/Role Instruction:**
"You are an experienced pharmacologist with extensive knowledge of drug interactions. Your task is to determine whether there is an interaction between the two drugs based on their pharmacological profiles. Specifically, you should consider the following factors:
- Pharmacodynamics: How the drugs affect the body, including their effects on receptors and physiological systems.
- Metabolic Pathways: How the drugs are metabolized, including enzyme interactions and potential effects on drug metabolism.
- Receptor Interactions: Whether the drugs interact with the same or similar receptors.
Task: Given the names and SMILES structures of two drugs, predict if there is an interaction between them. You should answer "yes" if there is an adverse interaction and "no" if there is no adverse interaction."

**Output Requirements:**
"Requirements:
- Prediction and Explanation: Provide a binary prediction ("yes" for an interaction or "no" for no interaction) and a concise explanation grounded in pharmacological evidence.
- Evidence-Based: Use reliable sources such as clinical trials, FDA labeling, drug interaction databases, and peer-reviewed literature to support your explanation.
- Structured Explanation: Clearly outline the reasoning for your prediction, addressing the pharmacological factors explicitly."

**Question Example (Template):**
```
Drug A Name: [Name of Drug A]
Drug A Smiles: [SMILES Structure of Drug A]
Drug B Name: [Name of Drug B]
Drug B Smiles: [SMILES Structure of Drug B]
Interaction Prediction: [yes/no]
Explanation: [Provide a detailed explanation referencing pharmacodynamics, metabolic pathways, receptor interactions, and clinical data.]
```
