# Artificial Intelligence in Oncology (Overview)

## Description

Artificial Intelligence (AI) in Oncology represents a revolution in the fight against cancer, spanning from **early detection** to **personalized treatment planning**. Its value lies in the ability to analyze vast datasets (medical images, genomic data, electronic health records) with speed and accuracy that surpass human capabilities, enabling faster and more accurate diagnoses and optimizing therapies such as radiotherapy. AI acts as a **cognitive assistant** for oncologists, pathologists, and radiologists, improving clinical efficiency and decision-making, which translates into better outcomes for patients. The field is evolving rapidly, with solutions ranging from clinical decision support systems (such as the ASCO Guidelines Assistant) to radiotherapy contouring and planning tools (such as the syngo.via RT Image Suite). Despite challenges such as the need for robust clinical validation and data integration, AI is fundamental to the next generation of precision medicine in oncology.

## Statistics

**Breast Cancer Diagnosis:** A multinational study presented at ASCO 2025 demonstrated that AI can increase the **overall accuracy** in diagnosing breast cancer subtypes (HER2-low/ultralow) from 66.7% to **88.5%** (a 22% increase in accuracy). AI reduced by more than **25%** the cases incorrectly classified as HER2-null, allowing more patients to access targeted therapies. **Radiotherapy:** AI is estimated to reduce the contouring time of Organs at Risk (OARs) in radiotherapy from hours to **minutes**, increasing planning efficiency. **Adoption:** 78% of oncologists believe AI will have a positive impact on radiotherapy. **Performance:** Some AI platforms reached **97% accuracy** in breast cancer diagnosis in early tests.

## Features

**Detection and Diagnosis:** Analysis of medical images (mammograms, CT scans, MRIs) to identify lesions and tumors with high sensitivity and specificity; Classification of tumor subtypes (e.g., HER2-low/ultralow) in digital pathology; Prediction of tumor aggressiveness (e.g., the PROTsi platform from USP). **Treatment Planning:** Automatic contouring of Organs at Risk (OARs) and tumor volumes (GTV/CTV/PTV) in radiotherapy; Optimization of dosimetry plans; Guideline-based clinical decision support (e.g., ASCO Guidelines Assistant); Image-guided adaptive radiotherapy (IGRT/MRgRT). **Precision Oncology:** Analysis of genomic data and biomarkers for the selection of targeted therapies and immunotherapies; Prediction of treatment response and toxicity.

## Use Cases

**1. Early Detection and Screening:** Use of AI to analyze mammograms, chest CT scans (for lung cancer), and colonoscopies, identifying suspicious nodules and lesions that may be overlooked by the human eye. **2. Digital Pathology:** Automated classification of biopsy slides to determine tumor grade and subtype, such as identifying HER2 status in breast cancer. **3. Radiotherapy Optimization:** Creation of faster and more precise radiation treatment plans, with automatic contouring of critical structures and real-time plan adaptation (Adaptive Radiotherapy) to compensate for patient movement or changes in tumor shape. **4. Personalized Oncology:** Analysis of genomic and clinical data to predict a patient's response to different chemotherapies, immunotherapies, or targeted therapies, aiding in the selection of the most effective treatment. **5. Clinical Decision Support:** Providing oncologists with fast, interactive access to evidence-based clinical guidelines and medical literature, such as the ASCO Guidelines Assistant.

## Integration

AI integration in Oncology occurs primarily through **dedicated software systems** that connect to the existing hospital infrastructure (PACS, RIS, EMR). \n\n**1. Integration with PACS/RIS (Radiology and Pathology):**\nAI tools are frequently integrated as modules in imaging workstations (e.g., syngo.via RT Image Suite) or digital pathology systems. They process the images (DICOM) and return results (contours, classifications) that are stored back in the PACS/RIS.\n\n**2. Integration with EMR (Electronic Medical Record):**\nDecision support systems (e.g., Watson for Oncology, ASCO Guidelines Assistant) access clinical data from the EMR to provide recommendations. Integration is typically via standardized APIs (such as FHIR) or secure web interfaces.\n\n**Integration Example (Conceptual - Python for Image Analysis):**\nAlthough most commercial solutions do not use open source, the principle of integrating a trained AI model (e.g., for tumor segmentation) into a research workflow can be illustrated:\n\n```python\nimport pydicom\nimport requests\n\n# AI service API URL (conceptual example)\nAI_SERVICE_URL = "https://api.ai-oncology.com/segmentation"\n\ndef processar_imagem_dicom(caminho_dicom):\n    # 1.
