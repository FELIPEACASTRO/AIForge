# Motion Design Prompts

## Description
Motion Design Prompts are detailed text instructions provided to video-generation Artificial Intelligence models (such as Runway Gen-2, Pika Labs, Sora, Veo, and Adobe Firefly) to create animations, motion graphics, and short videos. They differ from image prompts in that they must describe not only the static visual, but also the **action**, the **movement** of the subject and the **camera**, and **temporal consistency**. The ideal structure combines elements of image prompts with cinematographic and animation descriptions, focusing on: Subject, Action, Scene, Camera Movement, and Visual Style.

## Examples
```
1. **Logo Animation:** "A minimalist 3D fox logo, made of frosted glass, slowly rotates and disintegrates into purple smoke. Black background. Camera with a slow zoom (dolly zoom). Studio lighting."
2. **Cinematic Scene:** "An astronaut floating in zero gravity inside an abandoned space station. The camera performs a slow 'reveal' movement, starting from behind the astronaut's shoulder and revealing the Earth through the window. Cinematic style, dark and dramatic colors."
3. **Abstract Graphic:** "Particles of blue and gold light move in a smooth wave pattern, forming an exponential growth chart. Clean, futuristic 'motion graphics' style. Static camera."
4. **Stop-Motion Style:** "A small clay robot (claymation) walks clumsily across a wooden table. The camera follows the robot in a lateral 'pan'. Soft, warm lighting."
5. **Text Animation:** "The word 'FUTURE' appears in bright blue neon letters, with a glitch effect, and switches off. Close-up camera. Cyberpunk style."
6. **UI Prototyping:** "A loading animation of a circle that transforms into a green checkmark icon. White background. Smooth and responsive UI animation."
7. **Product Visualization:** "A high-tech running shoe rotates 360 degrees on a marble pedestal. The camera slowly orbits the shoe. Product lighting, sharp reflections."
8. **Fantasy Scene:** "A bioluminescent forest at night. Mushrooms and plants emit green and blue light. The camera moves through the foliage in a slow 'dolly'. Fantasy style, 4K."
```

## Best Practices
**Be Specific About Movement:** Use clear action verbs and camera movement descriptions (e.g., "dolly out", "tilt up", "slow pan"). **Define the Animation Style:** Specify whether it is "3D render", "2D cartoon", "stop-motion", "motion graphics", etc. **Maintain Consistency:** Repeat important keywords (such as the name of the main object) to help the model maintain visual coherence across frames. **Use Cinematic Terms:** Incorporate terms like "cinematic lighting", "wide shot", "macro lens" to refine the aesthetic. **Aesthetic Control:** Use adjectives for light, color, and texture (e.g., "dramatic lighting", "vibrant pastel colors").

## Use Cases
**Marketing and Advertising:** Rapid creation of video ads for social media. **Interface Design (UI/UX):** Prototyping micro-interactions and loading animations. **Content Production:** Generating intros, lower thirds, and transitions for YouTube or streaming videos. **Data Visualization:** Animating complex charts and infographics. **Art and Entertainment:** Creating music videos, conceptual short films, and generative art.

## Pitfalls
**Vague Prompts:** "A car driving" will result in something generic and without direction. **Excessive Movement:** Describing too many complex or fast movements can lead to artifacts and inconsistency. **Lack of Consistency:** Failing to clearly specify the style or subject can cause the object to change shape or color in the middle of the animation. **Ignoring the Camera:** Focusing only on the subject and forgetting to describe the camera movement (or lack thereof) results in static videos or ones with unwanted movement. **Overly Long Prompts:** Prioritize the most important terms, as excessively long prompts can confuse the model.
