# Omaha Math, Combinatorics & Equity (Matemática, Combinatória e Equity no Omaha)

> A deep, self-verifiable dive into the numbers that make Pot-Limit Omaha its own game: exact starting-hand combinatorics (combinatória das mãos iniciais), why equities run close (por que os equities ficam próximos), nut-focus and blockers (foco no nuts e bloqueadores), rundowns and 20-out wraps (rundowns e wraps de 20 outs), pot-limit bet-sizing math (matemática do limite do pote), running it twice and variance (rodar duas vezes e variância), and Hi-Lo low-hand counting. **Research and education only.** Every combinatorial figure on this page was computed from first principles and every equity figure was generated by open-source Monte-Carlo simulation for this page (method box below); every external link was opened live in July 2026.

## Why Omaha Math Is Different

In Omaha each player receives **four** hole cards (PLO4), five in 5-Card PLO (PLO5) or six in 6-Card PLO (PLO6), and — this is the pivotal rule — **must use *exactly two* hole cards plus *exactly three* community cards** to form the five-card hand ([Upswing — Omaha Hi-Lo rules](https://upswingpoker.com/poker-rules/omaha-hi-lo/): "must use *exactly* 2 hole cards to make their best 5 card high and low hand"). A single hole club on a four-club board is **not** a flush. That one constraint drives every number below.

Two consequences the rest of this page quantifies:

1. **Combinatorial explosion (explosão combinatória).** Four cards from 52 gives 270,725 starting combinations; evaluating one player's hand is a 60-way sub-problem, not a single lookup.
2. **Equity compression (compressão de equity).** With four cards, hands overlap in the outs they draw to, so preflop equities cluster together — the strongest hand is nowhere near the crush that Aces are in Hold'em.

## 1 · Starting-Hand Combinatorics (Combinatória das Mãos Iniciais)

The count of distinct dealt combinations is the binomial coefficient C(52, k) — computed exactly:

| Game | Hole cards | Starting combos C(52,k) | 5-card hands to evaluate **per player** |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| Texas Hold'em | 2 | C(52,2) = **1,326** | 1 (use both) |
| Pot-Limit Omaha (PLO4) | 4 | C(52,4) = **270,725** | C(4,2)·C(5,3) = **60** |
| 5-Card PLO (PLO5) | 5 | C(52,5) = **2,598,960** | C(5,2)·C(5,3) = **100** |
| 6-Card PLO (PLO6) | 6 | C(52,6) = **20,358,520** | C(6,2)·C(5,3) = **150** |

*The last column is the "exactly two of four, exactly three of five" cost: for every board you must test all C(hole,2)·C(5,3) two-card/three-card splits to find a player's best five. PLO evaluation is ~60× the work of a Hold'em lookup — one reason PLO solvers need heavy abstraction (see [Solvers & Open-Source Tools](../Solvers_and_Open_Source_Tools.md)).*

**Distinct *strategic* starting hands.** The 270,725 figure counts specific card sets; because the four suits are interchangeable before the flop, many are strategically identical (A♠A♥K♠K♥ plays exactly like A♣A♦K♣K♦). Collapsing hands under the 24 suit-relabelings (Burnside / canonical-form enumeration, computed for this page) leaves exactly **16,432 distinct PLO4 starting hands** — still 12× the 1,326 of Hold'em, and the real size of a preflop range chart.

**How rare is a premium?** A PLO4 hand containing at least a pair of Aces (any two of the four aces) occurs in 6,961 of the 270,725 combos = **2.57%** (about 1 in 39). Aces are **~5.7× more common** than in Hold'em (0.45%), which is part of *why* naked Aces are not the trophy they are in NLHE.

## 2 · Suit Structure: Double-Suited vs Single-Suited vs Rainbow

Suit shape is a first-class component of a PLO hand. Partitioning all 270,725 hands by suit pattern (exact counts computed for this page; they sum to C(52,4)):

| Suit pattern | Meaning | Combos | Share |
|---|---|---:|---:|
| **2-2** | **Double-suited** (two suits, two cards each → two flush draws) | 36,504 | **13.48%** |
| **2-1-1** | **Single-suited** (one two-card flush suit) | 158,184 | **58.43%** |
| **3-1** | Three of one suit (partly "dead" — you block your own flush outs) | 44,616 | 16.48% |
| **1-1-1-1** | **Rainbow** (no flush suit) | 28,561 | 10.55% |
| **4-0** | Monotone (all four one suit — worst suit shape) | 2,860 | 1.06% |

**Why double-suited (double-suited / "duas em dois naipes") is prized:** two flush draws plus many backdoor-flush combinations let you continue on more boards and *realize* equity more often. Upswing ranks the hand-quality inputs, in order, as **nuttiness → connectedness → suitedness**, and warns that holding **three or more cards of one suit weakens a hand "as you block your own flush draw outs"** ([Upswing — PLO starting hands](https://upswingpoker.com/pot-limit-omaha-starting-hands-plo-preflop-strategy/)). That is the 3-1 and 4-0 rows above: a third card of your suit is a card you can *no longer catch*.

Only **1 in ~7.4 hands is double-suited**, which is what makes AA**ds** and rundown**ds** holdings scarce enough to be premium.

## 3 · Why Equities Run Close (Compressão de Equity Multiway)

This is the defining mathematical fact of Omaha. In Hold'em, A♠A♥ is ~85% against a random hand — a crush. In PLO, the *best* starting hands barely clear 70%, and matchups between good hands are far tighter than Hold'em's blowouts — typically inside a 47–62% band, and a genuine coin flip when the two hands sit in the same tier.

**Heads-up, vs. a random hand** (single opponent):

| Hand | Type | Equity vs. random | Reading |
|---|---|---:|---|
| A♠A♥ (Hold'em) | NLHE baseline | **~85%** | The Hold'em "crush" |
| A♣A♦Q♣Q♦ (**AAQQds**) | AA + second pair, double-suited | **~71%** | About the ceiling in PLO4 |
| A♣A♦K♣K♦ (**AAKKds**) | premium double pair | **~71%** | Top-tier by *both* raw equity and playability (blocks AK/KK) |
| A♣A♦J♣T♦ (**AAJTds**) | AA + connected, double-suited | **~71%** | Statistically tied with AAKKds/AAQQds at the top |
| A♣A♦7♣2♥ (**AA72r**) | naked Aces, rainbow | **~64%** | Naked AA already lags the connected/double-pair AA-ds by ~7 points |
| 6♣5♣4♦3♦ (**6543ds**) | low rundown | **~44%** | Even a random hand is a small favorite — low made hands get outdrawn |

**Heads-up, good hand vs. good hand** — much closer than Hold'em, but rarely a literal 50/50 unless the tiers match:

| Matchup | Equity (first hand) |
|---|---:|
| AAKKds **vs** A♥A♠J♥T♠ (**AAJTds** — top tier vs top tier) | **~53%** (the true near coin flip) |
| J♥T♠9♥8♠ (**JT98ds**) **vs** A♣A♦7♣2♥ (**AA72r** — rundown vs *naked* aces) | **~47%** (rundown a slight dog) |
| AAKKds **vs** J♥T♠9♥8♠ (**JT98ds** — premium vs double-suited rundown) | **~61%** |
| KKQQds **vs** JT98ds | **~62%** |

Upswing states the rule directly: *"The best AAxx hands have ~70% pre-flop equity versus a random hand (AA has 85% in NLHE), and nearly every pre-flop matchup between good hands is 55/45 or similar"* ([Upswing — PLO strategy](https://upswingpoker.com/plo-poker-pot-limit-omaha-strategy/)). Our independent simulations reproduce the 85% and ~70% anchors; good-hand-vs-good-hand runs in a ~47–62% band — genuinely coin-flip (~47–53%) when the hands share a tier or when a rundown faces *naked* aces, and ~60/40 when a premium double-pair meets a rundown. Nothing here resembles Hold'em's 85/15 crush.

**Multiway equity compression (compressão multiway).** Because hands overlap, adding opponents crushes any one hand's share fast. AAKKds:

| Field (random opponents) | AAKKds equity | Fair share (1/N) |
|---|---:|---:|
| vs 1 | **~71%** | 50% |
| vs 3 | **~45%** | 25% |
| vs 5 | **~35%** | 16.7% |

The premium still beats its fair share (45% > 25%; 35% > 16.7%) — so it is +EV to be in — but its *absolute* share collapses, and it now needs to actually *make a hand*, not just hold. This is why PLO pots are contested multiway and swing hard: many hands have live, overlapping equity, so more chips go in as underdogs-with-outs. **This overlap is the source of the higher variance flagged in the responsible-gambling box.**

> **Method box (reproducibility / reprodutibilidade).** Equity figures here were generated for this page by Monte-Carlo simulation (≈150k–210k random run-outs per matchup; standard error ≈0.1% near 50%) using the open-source **[phevaluator](https://pypi.org/project/phevaluator/)** — the Python distribution of **[HenryRLee/PokerHandEvaluator](https://github.com/HenryRLee/PokerHandEvaluator)** — whose `evaluate_omaha_cards` function correctly enforces Omaha's exactly-two-of-four/three-of-five constraint (verified in-package: a lone suited hole card on a four-suit board does not make a flush). Wrap out-counts and wrap-versus-made-hand equities in §5 were computed by *exact* turn-and-river enumeration. Numbers are rounded to whole percent and cross-checked against Upswing's and PLO Genius's published anchors (e.g. our AAKKds-vs-AAJTds ≈ 53% and AAKKds-vs-KKQQds ≈ 69% reproduce PLO Genius's 52% and 69%). Any reader can reproduce them interactively with the **ProPokerTools Odds Oracle** ([propokertools.com](https://www.propokertools.com/)), which supports Omaha High, Omaha Hi/Lo 8-or-better and 5-Card Omaha.

## 4 · Nut-Focus & the Danger of Non-Nut Hands (Foco no Nuts)

The flip side of close equities is brutal **reverse implied odds (odds implícitas reversas)**: when you make a strong-but-not-nut hand, the money that goes in against you is disproportionately the nuts. Upswing puts it bluntly:

> *"It is (usually) criminal to not raise the river with the second or third nuts in NLHE; in PLO poker it is often suicidal if you do."* — [Upswing PLO strategy](https://upswingpoker.com/plo-poker-pot-limit-omaha-strategy/)

Consequences that fall straight out of the combinatorics:

- **Second-nut trap (armadilha do segundo nuts).** A king-high flush, a non-nut straight, or a low full house is a *paying* hand, not a *value-raising* hand, because with four cards per opponent the nut version is far more likely to exist than in Hold'em. Suited-K holdings and low rundowns like 6543 "can get you into problematic spots and should be approached with caution" ([Upswing — starting hands](https://upswingpoker.com/pot-limit-omaha-starting-hands-plo-preflop-strategy/)).
- **Nuttiness first.** Hands earn their value from making *the* nuts — nut flush (flush máximo), nut straight (sequência máxima), top set (trinca máxima) — or from being a multi-component hand where several draws point at the nuts. Non-nut hands "are always much stronger in a multi-component hand that has nuttiness in some direction" (Upswing).

### Blockers (Bloqueadores)

Because value and bluff frequencies hinge on whether the nuts is *possible*, **blockers matter more in PLO than in any other flop game**. Holding a card that removes an opponent's nut combination changes both your bluffing EV and your bluff-catching EV:

- **Nut-blocker bluffs:** holding the A♠ on a three-spade board means *you* block the nut flush — opponents can hold it far less often, so a bluff representing it succeeds more.
- **Draw redundancy is anti-blocking:** the 3-1/4-0 suit patterns from §2 are the same blocker logic — a third card of your flush suit is a card an opponent (and the board) cannot use, but it is also an out you cannot catch.

## 5 · Rundowns & Wraps: Counting Straight Outs (Rundowns e Wraps)

A **rundown (rundown / sequência conectada)** is four connected ranks (e.g. J-T-9-8); its power is the ability to flop a **wrap (wrap / projeto de sequência amplo)** — a straight draw that "wraps around" the board on both sides. Formally a wrap is **a straight draw with 9 or more outs**, and because you hold four cards, Omaha straight draws reach as many as **20 outs** — impossible in Hold'em ([CountingOuts — counting PLO wrap outs](https://www.countingouts.com/counting-plo-wrap-outs/)).

Worked examples on the same flop **9♥ 8♠ 2♦** (the two connected board cards are 9-8):

| Your hand | Structure vs. the 9-8 | Cards that complete a straight | Outs |
|---|---|---|---:|
| J♥ T♠ 7♦ 6♣ | two above **and** two below | 5(4) · 6(3) · 7(3) · T(3) · J(3) · Q(4) | **20** |
| J♥ T♠ 7♦ 4♣ | two above, one below | 6(4) · 7(3) · T(3) · J(3) · Q(4) | **17** |
| Q♥ J♠ T♦ 7♣ | three above, one below | 6(4) · 7(3) · T(3) · J(3) · Q(3) | **16** |
| Q♥ J♠ T♦ 4♣ | three above the two board cards | 7(4) · T(3) · J(3) · Q(3) | **13** |

*(Counts are exact remaining cards: e.g. a rank you already hold two of leaves 3 in the deck. Source examples: [CountingOuts](https://www.countingouts.com/counting-plo-wrap-outs/).)*

**Nut outs vs. raw outs (outs do nuts vs. outs brutos).** The single most important refinement: not every wrap out makes the *nut* straight. The 17-out J♥T♠7♦4♣ wrap above makes a straight with 17 cards but only **11 of them are nut-straight outs** (exact enumeration for this page); the other six make a straight that loses to a bigger one. Counting raw outs overstates a non-nut wrap — this is the §4 nut-focus principle applied to draws. For scale, the 20-out wrap above is a big favorite over an overpair (**~70%** with two cards to come) but only a slight favorite over top set (**~52%**, because the set redraws to full houses) — enumerated for this page. The *nut* out count, not the raw count, is what determines how much you can profitably commit.

## 6 · Preflop Hand Rankings (Ranking das Mãos Iniciais)

Combining §2–§4, the strongest PLO4 categories, best first:

| Tier | Archetype | Example | Why it ranks |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **AA double-suited, connected** | **AAJTds**, **AAKKds**, AAQQds | Top vs-random equity (~70–71%), two nut-flush routes, high-card + straight backup |
| 2 | **Premium double pairs / big broadway ds** | KKQQds, AKQJds | Nut/2nd-nut routes, robust multiway |
| 3 | **High double-suited rundowns** | JT98ds, KQJTds | Flop the biggest (often nut) wraps; run ~47% *even against naked aces* (though a clear dog to premium AAKKds) |
| 4 | Suited Aces with a side pair/connector | AKcc·xx | One nut-flush route + support |
| 5 | Mid rundowns, single-suited | 9876ss | Playable but more second-nut risk |
| — | **Avoid / trap-prone** | naked AAxx rainbow, low rundowns (6543), dangling cards (Q♦5♦ with an unconnected 9-8) | Little nuttiness, hard to realize the close equity |

**On the "best hand" question.** By raw heads-up equity vs. a random hand our simulations put the **connected/double-pair AA double-suited hands all at the top and statistically tied — AAQQds ≈ 71%, AAKKds ≈ 71%, AAJTds ≈ 71%** — matching Upswing's ~70% anchor. **AAKKds** is the traditional benchmark "best hand" in PLO folklore ([PLO Genius — best starting hands](https://content-blog.plogenius.com/omaha-best-starting-hands-a-quick-guide/)) not because it is uniquely highest against a random hand — it is level with its AA-ds siblings there — but because it *dominates realistic 3-bet ranges* (top set over top set, blocks KK/AK): our sims give it ≈ 53% vs AAJTds and ≈ 69% vs KKQQds, reproducing PLO Genius's 52% and 69%. A clean illustration that in PLO **playability and nuttiness, not just the pair, drive real hand value.** Notation: **ds** = double-suited, **ss** = single-suited, **r** = rainbow.

## 7 · Pot-Limit Bet-Sizing Math (Matemática do Limite do Pote)

Omaha is almost always played **pot-limit**: the maximum bet or raise equals the **amount to call plus the size of the pot *after* that call**. The at-the-table shortcut:

> **Max raise (total) = 3 × (last bet) + (pot before the last bet)** — equivalently, call the bet, then bet the new pot.

Worked $1/$2 example ([PLO Genius — how pot-limit works](https://content-blog.plogenius.com/pot-limit-omaha-what-does-pot-limit-imply/); arithmetic verified for this page):

| Spot | State | Max raise (to) | Check |
|---|---|---:|---|
| Preflop open (UTG) | SB $1 + BB $2 in pot; call $2 | **$7** | $2 call + $5 pot-after-call |
| Preflop 3-bet | Villain opened to $7; pot $10 | **$24** | $7 call + $17 pot-after-call · (3×$7 + $3 = $24) |
| Postflop | $10 bet into a $20 pot | **$50** | $10 call + $40 pot-after-call · (3×$10 + $20 = $50) |

**Why this compounds variance:** pot-limit lets the pot at least *quadruple* per street of pot-sized action (bet pot → caller makes pot ≈3×, next pot-sized bet is ≈3× that). Combined with §3's close equities, stacks go in with many live outs on both sides — the geometric-growth engine behind PLO's larger swings. (For the general geometric-sizing math, see [Game Theory & GTO Foundations — pot geometry](../Game_Theory_and_GTO_Foundations.md).)

## 8 · Running It Twice & Variance (Rodar Duas Vezes e Variância)

Because PLO all-ins are so often near coin-flips with big pots, **running it twice (rodar duas vezes)** — dealing the remaining board two times, each for half the pot — is standard in cash games. The math is clean and worth stating precisely:

- **It does *not* change EV (não muda o valor esperado).** *"The expected value is the same, regardless of how many times you run it"* — a 56.06%-equity hand books $56.06 of expected value on a $100 pot whether run once or twice ([GTO Wizard — insurance & running it twice](https://blog.gtowizard.com/poker-insurance-reducing-variance-or-wasting-money/); [PokerNews — why running it twice does not affect your odds](https://www.pokernews.com/strategy/why-running-it-twice-does-not-affect-your-odds-32880.htm)).
- **It *does* reduce variance (reduz a variância).** Splitting the pot across two independent-ish run-outs shrinks the spread of outcomes: it "reduce[s] negative variance" (fewer full-pot losses) at the cost of also reducing positive variance (fewer full-pot scoops) — pure variance reduction with **no house edge**, unlike EV-cashout/insurance products that skim a 1–2%+ fee (GTO Wizard).

Running it twice is the players' variance dampener; it does not make PLO a lower-variance *game* — it only smooths a single all-in. The structural variance from §3 and §7 remains.

## 9 · Hi-Lo Low-Hand Combinatorics (Combinatória da Mão Baixa no Omaha Hi-Lo)

**Omaha Hi-Lo (Eight-or-Better / "8 or better", O8)** splits the pot between the best high hand and the best *qualifying* low. Rules that set up the math ([Upswing — Omaha Hi-Lo rules](https://upswingpoker.com/poker-rules/omaha-hi-lo/)):

- A low **qualifies** only with **five distinct ranks all eight-or-lower**; straights and flushes do **not** count against the low, and the Ace plays low.
- Best low = **5-4-3-2-A (the wheel / "roda")**; worst qualifying low = **8-7-6-5-4**. If no one qualifies, the high hand **scoops (leva o pote inteiro)**.
- You still use **exactly two** hole cards for each half — and the two cards for the low can differ from the two for the high, so one four-card hand can attack both pots.

**The count.** Since suits are irrelevant to the low and the five ranks must be distinct and drawn from {A,2,3,4,5,6,7,8}, the number of distinct qualifying low hands is exactly:

> **C(8, 5) = 56 distinct low hands**, from 5-4-3-2-A (nut low) down to 8-7-6-5-4 (worst qualifier).

That tiny 56-hand ladder is why **nut-low focus (foco na nut low)** dominates Hi-Lo strategy: with so few rungs, drawing to a non-nut low invites getting **quartered (receber um quarto do pote)** — tying the low and taking only a quarter of the pot while paying full price. It is the §4 nut principle again, now on the low side.
