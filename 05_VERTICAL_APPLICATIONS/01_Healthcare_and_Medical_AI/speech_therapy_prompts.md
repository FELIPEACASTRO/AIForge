# Speech Therapy Prompts

## Description
**Speech Therapy Prompts** refer to the use of natural language commands (prompts) in Generative Artificial Intelligence models (such as ChatGPT, Gemini, etc.) to assist speech-language pathologists (speech therapists) in optimizing their clinical practice.

This prompting technique aims to automate repetitive tasks, generate highly personalized therapy materials, and support session planning. It is essential to distinguish this AI application from the established therapeutic method **PROMPT** (Prompts for Restructuring Oral Muscular Phonetic Targets), which is a tactile-kinesthetic approach for speech production disorders.

The effectiveness of AI prompts lies in the ability to personalize at scale, allowing the speech-language pathologist to create word lists, sentences, stories, or exercises that align directly with each patient's specific interests, skill level, and therapeutic goals, saving significant time in material preparation. The technique requires the professional to adopt the stance of a prompt engineer, providing detailed context and constraints to ensure the relevance and clinical accuracy of the generated material.

## Examples
```
**Example 1: Personalized Word List Generation (Articulation)**
```
Act as a speech-language pathology assistant. My patient is an 8-year-old boy with a speech sound disorder, working on the /r/ phoneme in word-initial position. He loves dinosaurs and rockets. Generate a list of 15 two-syllable words that begin with /r/ and are relevant to his interests. Format the output as a simple numbered list.
```

**Example 2: Creating Complex Sentences (Fluency/Language)**
```
Create 10 complex sentences for a 16-year-old adolescent with mild stuttering, focusing on words containing the /s/ and /z/ sounds. The sentences should be about the theme of video games and eSports. Include a breathing instruction before each sentence.
```

**Example 3: Clinical Report Draft (Documentation)**
```
Act as an experienced speech-language pathologist. Based on the following session data [INSERT DATA: e.g., "The patient (5 years old) demonstrated 80% accuracy in producing the /k/ phoneme in medial syllables. He maintained attention for 15 minutes and responded well to visual reinforcement."], draft the "Progress and Next Steps" section of a quarterly report. The tone should be professional and encouraging.
```

**Example 4: Activity Brainstorming (Planning)**
```
I am a speech-language pathologist working with a group of 4 children (6-7 years old) with expressive language delay. The goal of the session is to practice past-tense action verbs. Suggest 3 fun, low-cost activity ideas that I can carry out in 30 minutes, including the materials needed and a verbal prompt to start the activity.
```

**Example 5: Creating a Social Story (Pragmatics)**
```
Create a short social story for a 10-year-old patient with Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD) who has difficulty maintaining eye contact during conversation. The story should have 5 paragraphs, use simple language, and explain why eye contact is important and how to practice it in social situations. Include a title and a moral at the end.
```

**Example 6: Text Simplification (Comprehension)**
```
Take the following paragraph about photosynthesis [INSERT SCIENTIFIC TEXT] and rewrite it at a 3rd-grade reading level (8-9 years old). The vocabulary should be simple and the sentences short.
```

**Example 7: Generating Inference Questions (Receptive Language)**
```
Generate 10 medium-level inference questions about the children's book "Where the Wild Things Are". The questions should require the child to use text clues and world knowledge to answer.

## Best Practices
**1. Assign a Persona (Role-Playing):** Begin the prompt by instructing the AI to act as a "Speech-Language Pathologist Specialized in [Specific Area]" or "Speech-Language Pathology Assistant". This directs the tone, vocabulary, and depth of the response.
**2. Be Extremely Specific:** Include the therapeutic goal, the target audience (age, diagnosis, skill level), the sound/language structure to be worked on, and the patient's interests for personalization.
**3. Provide Examples (Few-Shot Prompting):** If the AI produces an unsatisfactory result, provide one or two examples of the format or type of content you want.
**4. Treat the AI as an Assistant:** Use the AI for drafting, brainstorming, and material generation tasks, but **never** for making clinical or diagnostic decisions. Professional review and judgment are indispensable.
**5. Iterate and Refine:** Do not accept the first response. Ask the AI to refine, correct errors, change the format, or increase/decrease complexity. (e.g., "Rewrite the list with two-syllable words only.")

## Pitfalls
**1. Confusing AI with Clinical Judgment:** The biggest mistake is delegating diagnosis, assessment, or treatment decisions to the AI. The AI is a support tool, not a substitute for clinical expertise.
**2. Privacy Violation (HIPAA/LGPD):** Never enter personally identifiable information (PII) or protected health data (PHI) of patients into the prompts. Use only anonymized or generic information (e.g., "8-year-old boy with dysarthria").
**3. Vague or Generic Prompts:** Using prompts like "Create a speech therapy activity" will result in generic materials with low clinical utility. Lack of specificity is the main cause of poor results.
**4. Accepting the First Response Without Review:** The AI can make factual, clinical, or formatting errors. The generated material **must** be reviewed by the speech-language pathologist before being used with the patient.
**5. Overdependence:** Blind reliance on AI can lead to atrophy of the professional's own planning and material-creation skills. The AI should complement, not replace, the speech-language pathologist's creativity and knowledge.
