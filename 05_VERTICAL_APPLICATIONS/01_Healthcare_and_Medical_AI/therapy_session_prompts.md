# Therapy Session Prompts

## Description
The **Therapy Session Prompts** technique refers to the strategic use of Prompt Engineering by mental health professionals (therapists, psychologists, coaches) or by individuals seeking self-knowledge and emotional support. The goal is to use Large Language Models (LLMs) as assistants to enhance the therapeutic process, from client assessment and treatment planning to developing exercises and simulating clinical scenarios. Effectiveness lies in the ability to 'prime' the AI with clear context, persona, and objectives, turning it into a versatile tool that can simulate different theoretical approaches (CBT, ACT, Gestalt, etc.) or assist with administrative and clinical reflection tasks [1] [2].

## Examples
```
## Therapy Session Prompt Examples

These prompts are designed to be used by therapists to support clinical practice or by individuals for guided self-reflection, covering different therapeutic goals [2].

1.  **Values Identification (ACT/Values-Focused Therapy):**
    > **Prompt:** "Act as a values-focused therapist. Create 5 powerful questions to help a client identify their 3 core life values and how they manifest in their daily actions."

2.  **Emotional Regulation (Compassion-Focused Therapy):**
    > **Prompt:** "Based on Compassion-Focused Therapy, write a 'self-compassion in failure' exercise (5 minutes long) for a client who is excessively self-critical and perfectionistic."

3.  **Thought Record (CBT):**
    > **Prompt:** "Act as a cognitive-behavioral therapist (CBT). Create a 'dysfunctional thought record' (DTR) table with columns for Situation, Automatic Thought, Emotion, Evidence For/Against, and Adaptive Response."

4.  **Breaking Patterns (Schema/Socratic Therapy):**
    > **Prompt:** "My client has a pattern of self-sabotage in relationships. Formulate 5 Socratic questions to help them explore the core beliefs and unconscious fears behind this pattern."

5.  **Goal Setting (Coaching/SMART):**
    > **Prompt:** "Use the SMART goal framework (specific, measurable, attainable, relevant, time-bound) to transform the vague objective 'I want to be happier' into a clear and actionable goal for the next 30 days."

6.  **Clinical Supervision Simulation:**
    > **Prompt:** "Act as an experienced clinical supervisor. The client showed resistance to discussing their relationship with their father. What theoretical approach (beyond the one I use) could give me a new perspective on how to address this challenge in the next session?"

7.  **Creating Metaphors:**
    > **Prompt:** "Create a short metaphor to explain the therapeutic process to a new client, describing it as a collaborative journey, focusing on the idea that the therapist is a guide, not a savior."
```

## Best Practices
Best practices for using prompts in a therapeutic context revolve around precision, context, and ethics [1].

*   **Clarity and Specificity:** Define the objective of the interaction unambiguously. Instead of asking for 'help with techniques', ask 'what are the strengths-based techniques for managing adolescent anxiety?'.
*   **Iterative Prompting:** Use dialogue to refine the response. If the first response is too general, use a follow-up prompt to go deeper or clarify the point.
*   **Task Segmentation:** Break complex tasks (such as a treatment plan) into smaller, sequential subtasks to obtain more focused and manageable results.
*   **Descriptive Language:** Use directive words such as 'summarize', 'critique', 'suggest', or 'elaborate' to shape the style and format of the AI's response.
*   **Use of Persona:** Ask the AI to adopt the persona of a theorist (e.g., 'Act as Carl Rogers') to gain insights from a specific theoretical perspective.
*   **Providing Ethical Context:** Provide enough context for the AI to generate relevant responses, but **NEVER** include PHI (Protected Health Information) or confidential client data to ensure compliance with HIPAA and privacy.

## Use Cases
The application of therapy session prompts is broad, benefiting both the professional and the client [1] [2].

*   **Exercise Development:** Rapid creation of self-knowledge, emotional regulation (mindfulness, self-compassion), and goal-setting (SMART) exercises.
*   **Treatment Planning:** Assistance in formulating individualized treatment plans, considering comorbidities and different theoretical approaches.
*   **Simulation and Role-Playing:** Therapists can use the AI to simulate difficult interactions or practice new therapeutic techniques, with the AI adopting the persona of a client with a specific challenge.
*   **Supervision and Perspective:** Obtaining a 'second opinion' or a new theoretical perspective on a challenging case, with the AI acting as a clinical supervisor.
*   **Reflection Tool for the Client:** Providing journaling or reflection prompts for the client to complete between sessions, deepening the therapeutic work.

## Pitfalls
The use of AI in therapeutic contexts requires caution due to ethical and quality risks [1].

*   **Privacy Violation (PHI):** The greatest risk is including Protected Health Information (PHI) in prompts, which violates regulations such as HIPAA and compromises client confidentiality.
*   **Lack of Awareness:** Relying on AI as if it had consciousness, empathy, or emotional understanding. AI operates through algorithms and does not replace the human therapeutic relationship.
*   **Overgeneralization:** Vague or context-free prompts can lead to generic, clinically irrelevant, or even inappropriate responses for the client's specific situation.
*   **Bias and Inaccuracy:** AI can perpetuate biases present in training data or provide clinically inaccurate information, always requiring validation and the therapist's professional judgment.
*   **Technology Dependence:** The risk of the therapist becoming overly dependent on AI for clinical reasoning, weakening their own assessment and intervention skills.
