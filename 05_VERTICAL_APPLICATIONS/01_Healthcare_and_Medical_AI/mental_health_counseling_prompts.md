# Mental Health Counseling Prompts

## Description
**Mental Health Counseling Prompts** are structured, intentional instructions given to Large Language Models (LLMs) to simulate interactions of emotional support, self-awareness, and cognitive processing, generally based on the principles of therapies such as Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT) or Positive Psychology.

These prompts are designed to guide the user through exercises in reflection, cognitive restructuring, values identification, and *mindfulness* techniques. They act as a tool for **low-risk, highly accessible emotional support**, helping to externalize thoughts, organize emotions, and practice self-compassion. It is crucial to emphasize that they are a **support** and not a substitute for the counseling or treatment of a licensed mental health professional. Effective use depends on the user's ability to provide detailed context and the AI's ability to maintain an empathetic, non-judgmental posture.

## Examples
```
**1. Thought Untangler (Anxiety/Overwhelm)**
\`\`\`
Act as a cognitive behavioral therapist. I am feeling overwhelmed by several disordered thoughts about [briefly list the topics]. Help me organize these thoughts into categories (e.g.: work, finances, relationships) and ask me one investigative question about the category that seems most urgent so that I can start thinking about a solution.
\`\`\`

**2. Circle of Control (Anxiety)**
\`\`\`
I am worried about [describe your main concern]. Help me apply Stephen Covey's "Circle of Control" concept. Create two lists: one with the aspects of this situation that are 'Within my control' and another with those that are 'Outside my control'. Then, suggest the first and smallest step I can take regarding an item that is within my control.
\`\`\`

**3. Inner Critic Reprogrammer (Self-Compassion)**
\`\`\`
My inner critic is telling me that "[write the criticism, e.g.: 'I am not good enough']". Act as a self-compassion therapist. Help me reframe this criticism into a kinder and more constructive observation. How would I speak to a dear friend who was going through the same situation?
\`\`\`

**4. Socratic Journal (Emotional Processing)**
\`\`\`
Today I am feeling [emotion, e.g.: 'a lot of anger'] because of [situation]. Act as a Socratic journal. Ask me 5 investigative questions that help me explore the root of this emotion, understand which need of mine was not met, and think of a constructive way to deal with it.
\`\`\`

**5. Positive Moments Hunter (Optimism/Gratitude)**
\`\`\`
I had a difficult day and my mind only focuses on what went wrong. Act as a positive psychology therapist. Help me do the exercise of "hunting for the 3 best moments of the day", however small they may be. Ask me questions so that I describe in detail a small positive moment that may have gone unnoticed.
\`\`\`

**6. Values Identification (Decision-Making)**
\`\`\`
I am at a moment of indecision about [describe the decision]. Act as a coach. Help me make this decision based on my values. Make a list of 15 universal values (e.g.: security, freedom, creativity) and ask me to choose the 5 most important to me. Then, help me analyze which of the options is most aligned with these 5 values.
\`\`\`

**7. Mindfulness Anchor (Grounding)**
\`\`\`
I am feeling disconnected and anxious. Guide me through a 2-minute text-based mindfulness exercise. Describe a simple step-by-step so that I can focus on my breathing and my 5 senses to reconnect with the present moment.
\`\`\`

**8. Evidence Collector (Impostor Syndrome)**
\`\`\`
I am suffering from impostor syndrome and feel that I do not deserve my success. Act as a coach and help me create an "evidence list" against this feeling. Ask me questions about my achievements, skills I have learned, and positive feedback I have received in recent years so that I can list concrete facts that counter the feeling of being a fraud.
\`\`\`

**9. Optimistic Reframing (Challenges)**
\`\`\`
I am facing a challenge: [describe the challenge]. My tendency is to see only the negative side. Act as a coach specializing in 'reframing'. Help me find 2 or 3 ways to see this same situation from a more optimistic perspective or as a learning opportunity.
\`\`\`

**10. Letter That Will Never Be Sent (Emotional Release)**
\`\`\`
I have many feelings bottled up regarding [person or situation]. Guide me through the exercise of writing a "letter that will never be sent". I will write everything I feel, without filters. Your role is only to encourage me to let it all out, ensuring that this is a safe space. At the end, congratulate me on the courage to express my feelings.
\`\`\`
```

## Best Practices
**1. Define the Role and Method:** Always start the prompt by defining the AI's role (e.g.: "Act as a cognitive behavioral therapist", "Be a self-compassion coach") and the methodology it should follow (e.g.: "Use the Circle of Control method", "Ask Socratic questions"). This ensures more structured and focused responses.

**2. Set Clear Boundaries:** Include a safety instruction, such as "Remember that you are an AI assistant and not a substitute for a licensed mental health professional. Do not make diagnoses."

**3. Provide Specific Context:** Instead of generalizing, describe the specific situation, emotion, or thought being processed. The more detail, the more relevant the response.

**4. Ask for Actions, Not Just Reflections:** Ask the AI to generate lists, action plans, breathing exercises, or to guide you through a visualization. This transforms passive reflection into an active tool for change.

**5. Use Cognitive Challenge Structure:** To deal with negative thoughts, ask the AI to act as a "devil's advocate" or to help find evidence that contradicts catastrophic thinking.

## Use Cases
**1. Low-Risk and Accessible Support:** Offering a first step toward self-awareness and emotional support for people who do not have immediate access or resources for traditional therapy.

**2. Non-Acute Crisis Management:** Helping users apply *grounding* and cognitive restructuring techniques during moments of anxiety or mild to moderate stress.

**3. Practicing Therapeutic Skills:** Using the AI to practice CBT (Cognitive Behavioral Therapy) exercises, such as identifying cognitive distortions and challenging automatic thoughts.

**4. Journaling and Guided Reflection:** Transforming the act of journaling into an interactive session, where the AI asks deepening questions (Socratic method) to explore the root of emotions.

**5. Strengthening Self-Compassion:** Creating exercises to reframe inner criticism and recognize personal strengths, helping to build a more positive and realistic self-image.

**6. Values-Aligned Decision-Making:** Assisting in the clarification of personal values so that the user can make complex decisions (career, relationships) with greater alignment and satisfaction.

## Pitfalls
**1. Mistaking Support for Therapy:** The most serious mistake is treating the AI as a licensed therapist. The AI has no real empathy, cannot diagnose, has no ethical responsibility, and should not be used in serious crises.

**2. Lack of Specificity:** Vague prompts like "Help me with my anxiety" result in generic and useless responses. Effectiveness depends on the detailed description of the situation, emotion, or thought.

**3. Privacy Violation:** Entering highly sensitive or identifiable health information into the prompt. Although AI providers have privacy policies, the user should always consider that the data is being processed by an external system.

**4. Over-Reliance:** Using the AI as the only support tool, avoiding seeking human professional help when necessary. The AI should be a bridge to self-awareness, not a substitute for treatment.

**5. Accepting "Hallucinations":** The AI may occasionally provide incorrect, dangerous, or clinically inaccurate advice. The user should always apply a critical filter and common sense, especially in health matters.
