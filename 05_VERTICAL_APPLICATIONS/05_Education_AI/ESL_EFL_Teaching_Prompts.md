# ESL/EFL Teaching Prompts

## Description
**ESL/EFL Teaching Prompts** (English as a Second Language/English as a Foreign Language) are structured and detailed instructions provided to large language models (LLMs), such as ChatGPT, to turn them into tools for instructional support and language practice. This technique is fundamental for educators and students, as it allows AI to act as a **personalized tutor**, a **conversation partner**, or an **instructional content creation assistant** [1] [2].

The effectiveness of these prompts lies in the ability to simulate authentic linguistic interactions and provide immediate, adaptive feedback. For the student, the technique enables the practice of specific skills (grammar, vocabulary, pronunciation, writing) in a safe, on-demand environment. For the teacher, the technique accelerates the creation of instructional materials, lesson plans, role-playing exercises, and personalized assessments [3].

The key to a successful ESL/EFL teaching prompt is **specificity**. The prompt should clearly define the AI's role, the student's proficiency level (A1 to C2), the task context, and the expected output format, ensuring that the AI's response is instructional, relevant, and aligned with the learning objectives [1]. The rise of this technique reflects the growing integration of AI into language education, transforming the way English is taught and learned globally.

## Examples
```
**1. Role-Playing and Conversation (Fluency Practice)**
`Act as a tour guide in London. I am a tourist who has just arrived and wants to know how to get to the British Museum. Keep the conversation at B1 level and use informal language. Start the conversation.`

**2. Writing Correction and Feedback (Grammar Practice)**
`Correct the following paragraph that I wrote. Then, identify and explain the most serious grammatical error I made and suggest a more advanced vocabulary word to replace "good". Paragraph: [Insert the student's paragraph here].`

**3. Instructional Material Creation (Teacher)**
`Create a 50-minute lesson plan for A2-level students on the use of the "Present Continuous" to describe future actions. Include a 10-minute game and a 15-minute writing exercise. The goal is for students to be able to write 5 sentences about their plans for the next weekend.`

**4. Vocabulary and Context Expansion**
`Explain the meaning and use of the phrasal verb "look up". Provide three example sentences in different contexts (formal, informal, and academic).`

**5. Job Interview Simulation (Specific Language Practice)**
`You are the hiring manager at a technology company. I am applying for a "Junior Developer" position. Ask me 5 typical interview questions. Keep the tone professional and at C1 level.`

**6. Pronunciation and Listening Practice (With Voice Tools)**
`Generate a short dialogue (4 lines) between two friends planning a dinner. The dialogue should include the words "definitely", "reservation", and "appetizer". I will read the dialogue aloud, and you should give me feedback on my pronunciation and intonation.`

**7. Cultural and Contextual Explanation**
`Explain the cultural difference between "football" (American football) and "soccer" (football) and how the sport is perceived in the US and the UK. Limit the response to 150 words.`

**8. Reading Comprehension Exercise Generation**
`Create a short text (200 words) at B2 level about the benefits of solar energy. Then, create 3 multiple-choice questions to test reading comprehension of the text.`
```

## Best Practices
**1. Be Specific and Contextualized (Role-Playing):** Always define the AI's role (teacher, native speaker, classmate, etc.), the student's proficiency level (A1, B2, C1), the topic, and the response format. This ensures that the AI's output is relevant and appropriate for the learning level. Example: "Act as a native British English speaker, B2 level, and ask me 5 questions about my vacation travel plans."
**2. Skill-Focused:** Direct the prompt toward a specific language skill (grammar, vocabulary, pronunciation, writing, reading, or conversation).
**3. Use Persona (Persona Prompting):** Ask the AI to adopt a persona (e.g., "You are an academic essay reviewer", "You are a tour guide in New York") to create authentic communication scenarios.
**4. Ask for Feedback and Correction:** Include clear instructions for the AI to provide constructive feedback, detailed grammatical corrections, or suggestions for more advanced vocabulary. Example: "Correct my paragraph and explain the most serious grammar error I made."
**5. Limit the Output (Output Constraints):** Use constraints such as "limit the response to 100 words", "use only the 'Present Perfect' tense", or "include 3 new vocabulary words" to keep the exercise focused.

## Use Cases
**1. Personalized Conversation Practice:** The AI acts as a 24/7 conversation partner, simulating role-playing dialogues (interviews, restaurant orders, negotiations) adapted to the student's level and interest [2].
**2. Rapid Generation of Instructional Materials:** Teachers can instantly create lesson plans, grammar exercises, contextualized vocabulary lists, and reading texts for different proficiency levels [3].
**3. Immediate Feedback on Writing:** Students receive detailed corrections of grammar, syntax, and style in their essays, enabling self-assessment and continuous improvement [1].
**4. Explanation of Complex Concepts:** The AI can break down complex grammar rules or subtle vocabulary differences (e.g., *say* vs. *tell*) into simple explanations with practical examples.
**5. Cultural and Contextual Immersion:** Using prompts to explore customs, traditions, slang, and cultural nuances of English-speaking countries, acting as a "cultural attaché" [2].
**6. Proficiency Exam Preparation:** Creation of test simulations (TOEFL, IELTS) or exercises focused on specific sections of the exam (e.g., writing argumentative essays).

## Pitfalls
**1. Vague or Generic Prompts:** Using prompts such as "Teach me English" or "Give me an exercise" results in broad, unhelpful responses. The lack of context (level, objective, format) dilutes the value of the interaction.
**2. Over-Reliance:** Students who use AI to generate complete answers instead of using it as a practice and correction tool may develop a dependence that harms the development of critical thinking and writing skills.
**3. Failing to Define the Proficiency Level:** Not specifying the level (A1, B2, etc.) can lead to responses that are too complex or too simplified, frustrating the student or failing to challenge them adequately.
**4. Ignoring Human Review:** The AI may make subtle factual or linguistic errors. Teachers and students should always review AI-generated material to ensure accuracy and cultural appropriateness.
**5. Not Asking for Specific Feedback:** Many students only ask for correction, but not for feedback on *why* the error occurred. This prevents deep learning. The prompt should request an **explanation** of the grammar rule that was violated.
