# Prompts for Theses/Dissertations

## Description
The technique of **Prompts for Theses/Dissertations** (Thesis/Dissertation Prompts) refers to the strategic application of Prompt Engineering to assist graduate students (Master's and PhD) and researchers in all phases of the academic writing process. Far from replacing the author's intellectual work, this technique positions Artificial Intelligence (AI) as a **highly specialized research and writing assistant**. The focus is on maximizing the efficiency, clarity, and methodological compliance of the work. The prompts are structured to delegate repetitive or organizational tasks to the AI, such as summarizing literature, suggesting methodologies, checking formatting (ABNT, APA, etc.), generating drafts of specific sections (such as the abstract or the introduction), and overcoming creative block. The effectiveness lies in the ability to provide the AI with a rigorous academic **role** and **context**, turning it into a virtual advisor, reviewer, or specialist in a specific subfield.

## Examples
```
**1. Generating Topics and Research Problem**
`Assume the role of a PhD Advisor specializing in [Field of Study, e.g., Artificial Intelligence and Ethics]. My general interest is [Broad Theme, e.g., the impact of AI on judicial decision-making]. Generate 5 original and relevant research problems for a thesis, focusing on recent research gaps (last 3 years). For each problem, suggest a suitable research methodology (qualitative, quantitative, or mixed).`

**2. Systematic Literature Review**
`Act as a Senior Journal Reviewer. Analyze the following article abstracts [PASTE THE ABSTRACTS HERE]. Identify the 3 main recurring themes, the most commonly used methodologies, and the main research gap that has not yet been addressed by these authors. Structure the response in table format.`

**3. Formulating Hypotheses and Objectives**
`Based on the following Research Problem: "[INSERT THE PROBLEM]", and my Object of Study: "[INSERT THE OBJECT]", formulate 3 clear and testable Research Hypotheses. Then, generate 1 General Objective and 3 Specific Objectives that align perfectly with the hypotheses and the central problem.`

**4. Draft of Methodological Section**
`Assume the role of a Methodologist. My study is [Type of Study, e.g., an exploratory case study] on [Theme]. Data collection will be done through [Collection Method, e.g., semi-structured interviews with 10 IT managers]. Data processing will be [Analysis Method, e.g., Thematic Content Analysis]. Write the draft of the "Methodological Procedures" section of my dissertation, ensuring that the language is formal, precise, and justifies the choices made.`

**5. Critique and Refinement of Argumentation**
`Act as a Critical Member of the Examining Committee. Analyze the following paragraph from my Results section: [PASTE THE PARAGRAPH]. My main conclusion is [INSERT THE MAIN CONCLUSION]. Point out the weaknesses in the argumentation, suggest where the evidence (data) needs to be more explicit, and rewrite the paragraph to make it more persuasive and academically rigorous.`

**6. Formatting and Style Check**
`Assume the role of an ABNT Standards Reviewer (NBR 6023 for references). Check the compliance of the following reference list. Correct any errors in punctuation, italics, or order of elements. If any essential information is missing, point out what it is: [PASTE THE REFERENCE LIST].`
```

## Best Practices
**Define the Role (Role-Playing):** Always begin the prompt by instructing the AI to assume a specific role, such as "Academic Advisor Specializing in Methodology", "APA 7th Edition Style Reviewer", or "Senior Researcher in [Your Field]". This drastically improves the quality and relevance of the response. | **Provide Detailed Context:** Academic writing is highly contextual. Include as much detail as possible: the exact theme, the target audience (examining committee, readers in the field), the stage of the work (proposal, review, writing), and the specific guidelines (ABNT, APA, Vancouver standards). | **Iteration and Refinement:** Use AI as a dialogue partner. Don't expect the perfect answer on the first prompt. Use the AI's output as the basis for the next prompt, asking for refinements, critiques, or expansions. For example: "Based on your methodology suggestion, now generate 5 interview questions for data collection." | **Fact-Checking (Double-Check):** Never blindly trust factual information, citations, or references generated by the AI. AI can "hallucinate" sources. Use it to *suggest* sources and concepts, but *always* verify the existence and accuracy of the references in reliable academic databases.

## Use Cases
**Research Proposal Development:** Generating proposal drafts, defining scope, and aligning objectives and rationale. | **Literature Review:** Summarizing complex articles, identifying trends and gaps in the literature, and organizing thematic references. | **Methodological Development:** Suggesting data collection and analysis methods (e.g., questionnaire design, interview guides, statistical techniques). | **Writing and Editing:** Generating drafts of sections (abstract, introduction, conclusion), refining clarity and academic tone, and checking compliance with formatting standards (ABNT, APA, etc.). | **Defense Preparation:** Simulating questions from the examining committee and assisting in structuring the oral presentation.

## Pitfalls
**Source Hallucination:** The most serious error is the AI inventing citations, authors, article titles, or URLs. The researcher must **always** verify the existence and accuracy of any suggested reference. | **Loss of Originality:** Over-reliance on AI for writing can lead to generic text, lacking the authentic voice and critical depth required in high-level work. AI should be used to *assist*, not to *replace* the writing. | **Violation of Integrity Policies:** Many universities have strict policies on the use of AI. Inappropriate use (e.g., submitting text entirely generated by AI without attribution) can be considered plagiarism or academic misconduct. | **Lack of Specific Context:** Vague or generic prompts (e.g., "Write an introduction about my topic") result in superficial outputs. The AI needs details about *your* work, *your* methodology, and *your* findings to be useful.
