# Product Launch Prompts

## Description
**Product Launch Prompts** are a specialized category of prompt engineering focused on leveraging Large Language Models (LLMs) to automate, accelerate, and enhance all phases of a product launch strategy (Go-to-Market - GTM). Instead of generic prompts, they are structured to extract highly contextual and actionable marketing, sales, public relations, and product outputs.

The technique is based on providing the AI with a detailed **product brief** (audience, USP, objectives) and requesting specific launch deliverables, such as content plans, sales funnel copy, press kits, and competitive analyses. This transforms the AI from a text generator into a **virtual launch strategist**, allowing marketing and product teams to create complex campaigns in a fraction of the traditional time. The effectiveness lies in the ability to simulate domain experts (copywriters, analysts, strategists) and request results that fit directly into a structured launch plan.

## Examples
```
**1. Complete Launch Strategy Blueprint**

```
You are an elite Product Strategist and Go-to-Market consultant. I'm launching a new product called [Product Name] in the [Industry/Niche] niche. Our target audience is [Target Audience] and our main differentiator is [Main USP]. Create a complete launch plan covering: 1) Pre-launch audience research, 2) 30-day content calendar, 3) Influencer/partner strategy, and 4) Key metrics to track during launch week. Present the plan in table format with daily actions, tools to use, and expected KPIs.
```

**2. Positioning and Messaging Matrix**

```
You are a Senior Brand Strategist. Help me develop a positioning and messaging framework for my product: [Product Name]. The product solves [Problem] for [Target Audience]. Create: 1) Positioning Statement, 2) Elevator Pitch, 3) 3 Tagline options, and 4) 5 Messaging Pillars with proof points. Present in a structured outline with tone/style suggestions for different channels (LinkedIn, X, Landing Page).
```

**3. Launch Funnel Copywriting (Email Sequence)**

```
You are a top-tier direct response copywriter. Create a complete funnel launch email sequence for [Product Name] designed for [Audience Type]. Include: 1) Teaser Email (3 days before), 2) Announcement Email (Launch Day), and 3) Closing Email (24h before the offer ends). Each email should follow my tone: [Describe the tone, e.g., casual-educational, bold-inspirational]. Use emotional triggers and logical flow to maximize conversions.
```

**4. Public Relations Angle Generator and Press Kit**

```
You are a Public Relations Strategist for technology launches. I'm launching [Product Name] on [Launch Date]. Generate a press kit outline, including: 1) Press release headline and structure, 2) 3 media story angles (trend, founder, innovation), 3) Founder bio summary, and 4) Contact pitch email for journalists. Use a professional but incisive tone, suitable for tech/startup media.
```

**5. Pre-Launch Engagement and Buzz Plan**

```
You are an expert in community growth and social launch strategy. I want to create massive buzz 30 days before launching [Product Name]. Develop a 30-day pre-launch content and engagement calendar. Include: 1) Storytelling topics, 2) Community-building posts, 3) Creator collaborations, and 4) Metrics to measure virality. Present everything in a structured weekly table with post types, platform, and expected results.
```
```

## Best Practices
**1. Provide Complete Context (The Briefing Method)**: Always include as much detail as possible: product name, target audience, unique value proposition (USP), desired tone of voice, and the specific goal of the launch (e.g., sales, sign-ups, buzz). The AI model is not a marketing expert without your input.

**2. Use the Funnel Structure (Tease, Announce, Close)**: Structure your prompts to cover all phases of the launch: pre-launch (buzz generation), launch (announcement and sales), and post-launch (retention and feedback).

**3. Assign an Expert Persona (Role-Playing)**: Begin the prompt by defining the AI's role (e.g., "You are a Senior-Level Direct Response Copywriter," "You are a Public Relations Strategist for Tech Startups"). This improves the quality and focus of the output.

**4. Request Structured Formats**: Ask for output in easy-to-use formats, such as tables, numbered lists, or JSON. This facilitates review and implementation (e.g., "Present the plan in table format with daily actions and KPIs").

**5. Iterate and Refine (Prompt Chain)**: Don't expect the perfect result on the first try. Use the output of one prompt as input for the next. For example, use one prompt to define the **Message** and another to generate the **Copy** based on that message.

## Use Cases
**1. Go-to-Market (GTM) Strategy Generation**: Quickly create a high-level launch plan, including timelines, channels, and KPIs, instead of starting from scratch.

**2. Messaging and Positioning Development**: Define the Unique Value Proposition (USP), Elevator Pitch, and Messaging Pillars to ensure consistency across all marketing materials.

**3. Sales Funnel Content Creation**: Generate copy drafts for landing pages, email sequences (teaser, announcement, closing), video scripts, and paid ad hooks.

**4. Rapid Competitive Analysis**: Simulate a competitive intelligence analysis to identify market gaps and differentiation angles for the new product.

**5. Public Relations (PR) Automation**: Create press release outlines, press kits, and pitch emails for journalists, accelerating the media outreach process.

## Pitfalls
**1. Over-Reliance on AI (The 'Single Prompt' Myth)**: Believing that a single prompt can generate a complete and perfect launch strategy. AI is an acceleration tool, not a substitute for human review, market validation, and strategic decision-making.

**2. Lack of Specific Context**: Using generic prompts without providing crucial details about the product, target audience, competitors, and differentiators. This results in generic and unusable outputs.

**3. Ignoring Brand Voice**: Not specifying the brand's tone of voice and personality. The AI can generate technically correct copy that does not resonate with the company's identity.

**4. Not Iterating or Refining**: Accepting the AI's first output. The best results come from an iterative process, where the output of one prompt is refined or used as input for the next.

**5. Confirmation Bias**: Using AI only to confirm existing ideas rather than exploring new or challenging angles (e.g., "Ask the AI to analyze the weakness of your product idea before launching").
