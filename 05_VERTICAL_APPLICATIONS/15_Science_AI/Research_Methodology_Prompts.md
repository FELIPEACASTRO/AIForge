# Research Methodology Prompts

## Description
The **Research Methodology Prompts** technique consists of structuring commands for language models (LLMs) with the goal of assisting in all stages of the scientific research process, from initial planning to data analysis. Instead of generic commands, these prompts are designed to assign the LLM a **specific role** (e.g., methodological consultant, experimental designer, statistician) and to provide **detailed context and constraints** about the research objective, variables, population, and type of study. The focus is to transform the LLM into a research assistant that helps map out the methodology, refine questions, design protocols, and plan the analysis, ensuring that the foundation of the work is solid and well justified.

## Examples
```
1. **Method Selection**: "You are a methodological consultant. Based on the research objective: [Objective], suggest the most appropriate method (quantitative, qualitative, or mixed). Justify your choice and explain how it meets the objective."
2. **Experimental Design**: "Act as a research designer. Develop a detailed experimental protocol for the study described: [Study Description]. Include control conditions, variables, randomization strategy, and measurement plan."
3. **Research Questions**: "Create a set of 10 research questions for a questionnaire aligned with the objective: [Objective]. Ensure clarity, neutrality, and suggest the most appropriate type of response scale (e.g., 5-point Likert)."
4. **Sampling Strategy**: "Based on the population [Target Population] and the objective [Objective], recommend a sampling strategy. Include the suggested sample size, the selection method (e.g., stratified, convenience), and the justification."
5. **Methodological Limitations**: "Identify and explain three potential methodological limitations in the study [Study Description]. For each one, suggest a mitigation strategy or how they should be addressed in the discussion."
6. **Statistical Analysis**: "You are a statistical consultant. Given the study design [Design], the variables [Variables], and the sample size [Size], recommend the most appropriate statistical tests (e.g., ANOVA, Regression, T-Test) and justify the choice."
7. **Qualitative Coding Framework**: "Based on the following qualitative research questions [Questions] and a data sample [Data Sample], generate a thematic coding framework. Include main themes, subthemes, and code examples."
```

## Best Practices
1.  **Define the Role and Objective**: Start by assigning a professional role to the LLM (e.g., "You are a statistical consultant") and link the prompt to a clear and specific research objective.
2.  **Add Context and Constraints**: Always include as much context as possible, such as variables, sample size, type of study (quantitative, qualitative, mixed), and target audience. Vague prompts result in vague responses.
3.  **Structure the Output**: Ask the LLM to structure the response in specific formats (e.g., table, numbered list, step-by-step protocol) to facilitate review and application.
4.  **Review and Refine Critically**: Treat the LLM output as a first draft or suggestion. Use your critical judgment to adjust, polish, or rework the material, especially on issues of validity and complex nuances.
5.  **Iterate and Test**: *Prompting* is a skill. The more you test and adjust your prompts, the more value you will extract at each phase of your research.

## Use Cases
1.  **Method Selection**: Suggest and justify the most appropriate research method (quantitative, qualitative, or mixed) based on the objective.
2.  **Experimental Design**: Develop detailed experimental protocols, including control conditions, variables, randomization strategies, and measurement plans.
3.  **Collection Instruments**: Create sets of questions for questionnaires and interviews, ensuring clarity, neutrality, and appropriate response scales.
4.  **Sampling Strategies**: Recommend the sample size, the selection method (e.g., random, stratified), and the justification based on the population and the objective.
5.  **Data Analysis**: Plan statistical analyses (recommend tests and justify) or suggest qualitative analysis approaches (e.g., grounded theory, narrative analysis).
6.  **Limitation Mitigation**: Identify potential methodological limitations and suggest mitigation strategies to strengthen the validity of the study.
7.  **Framework Development**: Generate thematic coding *frameworks* for qualitative data analysis.

## Pitfalls
1.  **Replacement of Critical Thinking**: Relying on the LLM to validate findings or interpret complex nuances. The LLM is a tool for organization and suggestion, not a substitute for academic rigor and the researcher's judgment.
2.  **Insufficient Input**: Providing short or generic prompts that do not include the necessary context, constraints, and data, resulting in superficial or irrelevant outputs.
3.  **Accepting the Output as Final**: Treating the LLM response as the final work. The output should always be considered a draft that requires human review, validation, and refinement.
